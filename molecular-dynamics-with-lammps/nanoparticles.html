What is a nanoparticle?

A nanoparticle is any material whose characteristic dimension falls in the range of 1–100 nanometres — one to one hundred billionths of a metre. This places them in the regime between isolated atoms or small molecules and bulk condensed matter, a territory where neither atomic quantum chemistry nor classical thermodynamics alone gives a complete picture.

To appreciate why that narrow size window is special, consider what changes as you subdivide a macroscopic object. Imagine a 1 cm³ gold cube. The overwhelming majority of its atoms sit deep in the interior, shielded from the environment by layer upon layer of neighbours. Now divide that same volume into ever-smaller pieces. The total volume stays fixed, but the total exposed surface grows — steeply, then explosively.

"A nanoparticle is a material where surface physics has overthrown bulk physics."

For a sphere of radius \(r\), surface area scales as \(r^2\) and volume as \(r^3\), so the surface-to-volume ratio grows as \(1/r\). Reduce \(r\) by a factor of 1000 — from a millimetre grain to a nanometre particle — and the ratio increases by exactly 1000. In a 5 nm gold particle, roughly 40% of all atoms reside on the surface. Compare this to a 1 cm gold cube where the surface fraction is \(\sim 10^{-7}\).

Surface-to-volume ratio · sphere
\[ \frac{S}{V} = \frac{4\pi r^2}{\tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3} = \frac{3}{r} \]

This inverse dependence on radius is the single most important relationship in nanoscience. Every other nano-effect — thermodynamic, electronic, optical, magnetic — is, to first approximation, a consequence of this geometry.

Thermodynamic consequences

Surface atoms sit in a fundamentally different energetic environment than interior atoms. Each interior atom in an FCC metal is coordinated by 12 nearest neighbours; a surface atom has only 9 (or fewer at edges and corners). Those missing bonds represent unsatisfied, higher-energy states — what chemists call dangling bonds and physicists describe as a local increase in free energy.

The result is a well-defined surface energy \(\gamma\) (energy per unit area, units J m⁻²). For a bulk object this is negligible. For a nanoparticle, where the surface-to-volume ratio is enormous, surface energy becomes a significant — sometimes dominant — contribution to the total free energy of the system.

Size-dependent melting · Gibbs–Thomson

The most striking thermodynamic consequence is melting point depression. For bulk gold: \(T_m = 1064\,°\text{C}\). For a 2 nm gold nanoparticle: experimentally observed values near \(300\,°\text{C}\) — a depression of over 700 degrees.

The Gibbs–Thomson (or Pawlow) relation quantifies this effect. At the solid-liquid transition, the free energy cost of the additional surface area destabilises the solid phase, shifting the equilibrium melting temperature downward:

Gibbs–Thomson relation
\[ T_m(r) = T_m(\infty)\left(1 - \frac{2\gamma_{sl}}{\rho_s L r}\right) \]

where \(\gamma_{sl}\) is the solid-liquid interfacial energy, \(\rho_s\) is the density of the solid, \(L\) is the bulk latent heat of fusion, and \(r\) is the particle radius. The correction term scales as \(1/r\) — consistent with the surface-to-volume picture. Smaller particle, larger surface contribution, lower melting point.

Sintering and coarsening

The thermodynamic instability of surfaces is also the driving force behind sintering (nanoparticles fusing together when heated) and Ostwald ripening (small particles dissolving and large ones growing, because the higher chemical potential of small particles makes them more soluble). Both processes are unwanted side-effects of surface energy minimisation in applications where you need the nanoparticle distribution to remain stable over time.

Quantum confinement

Alongside the thermodynamic story runs a purely quantum-mechanical one. In bulk metals and semiconductors, electrons occupy energy bands — quasi-continuous ranges of allowed energies arising from the periodic potential of the crystal lattice and the enormous number (\(\sim 10^{23}\)) of overlapping electron wavefunctions.

As particle size shrinks toward the electron's de Broglie wavelength (\(\lambda = h/p\), typically 1–10 nm in a semiconductor), the electron is no longer free to propagate in all directions. It is spatially confined, and confinement in quantum mechanics always discretises the energy spectrum.

Particle in a box · the minimal model

The simplest model — an electron trapped in an infinite square well of length \(L\) — already captures the essential physics:

Energy levels · infinite square well
\[ E_n = \frac{n^2 \hbar^2 \pi^2}{2 m_e L^2}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots \]

The level spacing \(\Delta E \propto 1/L^2\) — shrink \(L\) by a factor of 2, quadruple the spacing between levels. For \(L \sim 5\,\text{nm}\) and a semiconductor effective mass, \(\Delta E\) is large enough to produce visible-range photon emission. This is the operating principle of quantum dots.

Quantum dots · colour from confinement

A quantum dot is a nanocrystal small enough that quantum confinement determines its optical gap. Because \(\Delta E \propto 1/r^2\) (with corrections from the effective mass and dielectric environment), the emission wavelength is directly tunable by changing the particle size — no chemical reformulation required, just size control.

Beyond the square well

Real nanoparticles are spherical, not cubic, and electrons experience a finite potential barrier (not infinite) at the surface. The correct model is the spherical particle in a finite spherical well, solved by matching Bessel function solutions inside and outside the particle. The result is the same qualitative trend — discrete energy levels growing further apart as \(r\) decreases — but with corrections for finite barrier height and wavefunction leakage into the surrounding medium.

Strong confinement also mixes electron and hole states and enhances the electron-hole exchange interaction, making selection rules and oscillator strengths strongly size-dependent. This is why quantum dot fluorescence lifetimes, quantum yields, and Stokes shifts all vary with particle size.

Beyond surface and quantum effects

Thermodynamics and quantum confinement are the two dominant physics stories at the nanoscale, but they are not the only ones.

Plasmonics

In metallic nanoparticles (particularly gold and silver), the free electrons can oscillate collectively in resonance with an incident electromagnetic wave. This is the localised surface plasmon resonance (LSPR). For a 20 nm gold sphere, LSPR occurs near 520 nm — the familiar ruby-red colour of colloidal gold. The resonance frequency, linewidth, and near-field enhancement all depend on particle size, shape, and the surrounding dielectric. This is why gold nanoparticle solutions can appear red, purple, or blue by varying particle size alone.

Mie scattering · extinction cross-section (dipole term)
\[ C_\text{ext} \approx 9\frac{\omega}{c}\varepsilon_m^{3/2} V \frac{\varepsilon_2}{(\varepsilon_1 + 2\varepsilon_m)^2 + \varepsilon_2^2} \]

where \(\varepsilon_1 + i\varepsilon_2\) is the complex dielectric function of the metal, \(\varepsilon_m\) is the (real) dielectric constant of the medium, and \(V\) is the particle volume. The resonance condition \(\varepsilon_1 = -2\varepsilon_m\) defines the LSPR frequency.

Superparamagnetism

Bulk ferromagnets are divided into magnetic domains — regions of uniform magnetisation separated by domain walls. Forming domain walls costs energy; the equilibrium domain structure balances this cost against magnetostatic energy. Below a critical particle radius \(r_c \sim 10\text{–}100\,\text{nm}\) (material-dependent), it costs more energy to form a domain wall than to remain single-domain.

In a single-domain particle, thermal fluctuations at room temperature can be enough to flip the entire magnetisation direction if the anisotropy energy \(K V k_B T\) — this is superparamagnetism. The particle behaves like a paramagnet with a giant (super) magnetic moment, saturating in an applied field but retaining zero remanence when the field is removed. This is the working principle behind MRI contrast agents and magnetic hyperthermia for cancer treatment.

Mechanical properties

The yield strength and hardness of nanocrystalline metals (grain sizes below ~100 nm) can be many times higher than their coarse-grained counterparts — a consequence of grain-boundary strengthening (the Hall–Petch relation) and the suppression of dislocation sources within nanoscale grains. Interestingly, at very small grain sizes (\(<\sim 10\,\text{nm}\)), the relation inverts (inverse Hall–Petch): hardness decreases because grain-boundary sliding and diffusion creep become the dominant deformation mechanisms rather than dislocation glide.

Why any of this matters

The properties that make nanoparticles scientifically interesting are also what make them technologically useful. A partial inventory:

Catalysis. The fraction of catalytically active surface atoms scales as \(1/r\). Platinum nanoparticles in a fuel cell catalyst layer expose far more active sites per gram than bulk platinum, dramatically reducing the loading (and cost) required.

Medicine. Gold nanoparticles can be functionalised with antibodies or peptides to target specific cell types. Their strong LSPR absorption converts near-infrared light to heat, enabling selective photothermal ablation of tumours. Iron oxide nanoparticles provide contrast in MRI and, under an oscillating magnetic field, deposit heat selectively in tumour tissue (magnetic hyperthermia).

Displays and lighting. Quantum dots with sharp, tunable emission and near-unity quantum yields have entered commercial display technology (QLED panels) and are under investigation for next-generation LED lighting.

Energy. Nanostructured electrodes increase the surface area available for electrochemical reactions in batteries and supercapacitors. Semiconductor nanocrystals are explored as solar cell absorbers whose bandgap can be tuned to match the solar spectrum.

Nanoparticles are not small versions of bulk materials. They are a different phase of matter — one where geometry rewrites the physics.
