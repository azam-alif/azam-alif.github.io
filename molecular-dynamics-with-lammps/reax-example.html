Testing ReaxFF in LAMMPS

Home  Blog Topics 

Introduction

Reactive Force Field (ReaxFF) is a molecular dynamics force field designed for simulating chemical reactions. Unlike traditional force fields where bonding remains fixed throughout the simulation, ReaxFF dynamically updates bond orders, allowing bonds to continuously break and form.

In this work, a simple H–C–O system was simulated using LAMMPS to test the ReaxFF implementation, trajectory generation, energy evolution, and species analysis. The goal is not to model a realistic material, but to verify that the reactive force field behaves correctly during a molecular dynamics simulation.

Simulation Setup

The simulation contains 30 atoms distributed inside a cubic simulation box of size

\[ 25 \times 25 \times 25 \ \text{Å}^3 \]

consisting of:

• 10 Hydrogen atoms

• 10 Carbon atoms

• 10 Oxygen atoms

Periodic boundary conditions were applied in all directions, and the simulation used the ReaxFF CHO potential with dynamic charge equilibration.

Initial atomic velocities were assigned at 1000 K using a Gaussian distribution, while the temperature was maintained using the NVT ensemble.

The simulation timestep was

\[ \Delta t = 0.25\ \text{fs} \]

and the total simulation length was

\[ 200000 \times 0.25\ \text{fs} = 50\ \text{ps} \]

Energy Evolution

During the simulation, thermodynamic quantities such as temperature, kinetic energy, potential energy, and total energy were monitored.

In ReaxFF simulations, the potential energy continuously changes because the bonding environment evolves dynamically as atoms move and interact. This behavior is different from classical fixed-bond molecular dynamics.

Trajectory Visualization

Atomic trajectories were exported from LAMMPS and visualized using OVITO. The trajectory animation shows atoms moving throughout the simulation box while dynamically interacting under the reactive force field.

Species Analysis

One of the most useful features of ReaxFF is automatic species analysis. During the simulation, LAMMPS continuously tracks temporary molecular structures formed between atoms.

Initially, the system starts with isolated groups of H, C, and O atoms. However, after the simulation begins, the species analysis shows the rapid formation of molecular species such as:

• H2

• O2

• Isolated oxygen atoms

Interestingly, the carbon atoms largely remain grouped as a stable C10 cluster throughout the simulation, while hydrogen and oxygen atoms reorganize into smaller molecular structures.

The species output also shows that the system rapidly stabilizes after the initial reactive stage. After approximately the first few hundred timesteps, the dominant molecular populations remain nearly constant, indicating that the system has reached a relatively stable reactive configuration at 1000 K.

Why ReaxFF is Important

Traditional molecular dynamics force fields assume fixed connectivity between atoms. ReaxFF removes this restriction by dynamically calculating bond orders from interatomic distances.

Because of this, ReaxFF can simulate:

• Bond breaking

• Bond formation

• Charge redistribution

• Reactive chemical processes

This makes ReaxFF useful for studying combustion, oxidation, catalysis, battery chemistry, and material degradation.

Conclusion

This simple ReaxFF simulation demonstrates how reactive molecular dynamics can be performed in LAMMPS using a small H–C–O system.

Using this setup, we can:

• Test the ReaxFF force field

• Visualize reactive atomic motion

• Monitor energy evolution

• Analyze molecular species formation

Even though this example is intentionally simple, the same workflow can be extended to realistic reactive systems involving combustion, catalysis, oxidation, and materials science.

All simulation files, analysis scripts, and visualization tools are available at GitHub.
